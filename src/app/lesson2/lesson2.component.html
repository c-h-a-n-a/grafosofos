Different Categories in Campus Journalism

Campus Journalism serves as a vital platform for students to express their thoughts, respect the truth, provide news and information, and engage with peers within the social community. It is one of the avenues that provides students an opportunity to practice their writing skills not just creatively, but also critically, and at the same time nurturing their moral character and personal discipline as Filipino youth.

On July 5, 1991, the Philippine Government approved and passed the Republic Act 7079 referred to as the Campus Journalism Act of 1991. This act safeguards the freedom of the press even within campus level or educational institutions that aim to support the improvement of journalistic skills of students and advocate for the preservation of the integrity of journalism.

Within this dynamic field, various categories exist, each playing a unique role in shaping the narrative of campus life. By engaging in meaningful storytelling and upholding the highest standard of journalism, offers students the opportunity to explore different facets of storytelling and communication. In this lesson, we will delve into the different categories that make up the vibrant landscape of campus journalism. Here are the categories in campus journalism:

1. News Writing

— News writing is a form of journalism that aims to provide readers with factual information about a story in an objective and unbiased manner.

— In news writing, the language used must be clear and concise and the presentation of information must be complete, correct, cohesive, and chronological—these are the six C’s of news writing.

— When engaging in journalistic writing, it is essential to consider both the audience and the tone in which the piece is delivered, as well as the ABCs of news writing: Accuracy, Brevity, and Clarity. (NMU Writing Center, 2024)

- ABCs of News Writing

  • Accuracy: in news reporting is crucial as it upholds public trust, prevents potential libel issues, and underscores the importance of thorough fact-checking for journalists to maintain credibility and integrity in their work.

  • Brevity: Get straight to the point. A news story must get to the point quickly and concisely. Your lead should draw your reader in and you should end with an interesting finish.

  • Clarity: You should have all of your facts and have them organized before you start writing. Your story should leave no question unanswered and should avoid jargon (in other words, make the topic accessible for readers of all levels, not just experts).

- News Value: What Makes a Story Newsworthy?

The process of news writing encompasses not only reporting important events and providing facts but also determining the relevance and impact of a story. This will help you to decide whether or not to cover an event or an announcement and to capture the attention of audiences or readers. News has value when it shows at least one of the following elements:

  • Prominence: Events are more newsworthy when they involve public figures.

  • Proximity: Events are more newsworthy the closer they are to the community reading about them.

  • Timeliness: An event is more newsworthy the sooner it is reported.

  • Impact: Events are more newsworthy when they affect a greater number of people and it makes a difference in the lives of the readers.

  • Conflict: Events are more newsworthy when they involve disagreement.

  • Human Interest: News that is touching, stirring, or otherwise interesting has news value.

  • Novelty/Oddity: Events are more newsworthy the more out of the ordinary they are.

- The Structure of the News Story

News articles follow an Inverted Pyramid format. The article's lead, or main points—the who, what, when, where, why, and how of a story—are placed at the top or beginning of the article. Additional information follows the lead and less important, but still relevant information, comes after that. The lead information gets the most words since many people read the lead and then skim the rest of the article.

The Lead:

The lead could be seen as the most essential part of the newspaper structure. It should be one paragraph long, and by reading it, your audience should be able to understand exactly what your article is about and what you are reporting on.

Key features of the lead paragraph:

  • It needs to be short and snappy so that the message is portrayed clearly. You can achieve this by communicating your message in as few words as possible- just make sure it makes sense.

  • The lead paragraph should explain clearly what has happened so that if your readers stopped reading after the lead, they would understand the main message of your report.

  • Stick to using the past tense.

  • It should be written in the third person.

The Five Ws (and H)

Another way to make sure that your reader gets the key messages in your report is to stick to the Five Ws in your lead paragraph.

These include:

  • Who: which people were mainly affected by the events you are reporting on?

  • What: what are the key events that happened?.

  • Where: where did those events take place?

  • Why: is there a specific reason why these events occurred?

  • When: what was the time, day, month, and year (if necessary) that the events happened?

  • How: this isn’t strictly a word starting with the letter ‘w’, but it is useful for your lead paragraph. Explain the manner in which the events occurred, or what made them able to happen.

The Body:

This is the main section of your newspaper article, so it will include lots of important information about what happened, along with more detail about what you said in your lead section. The body should be around 3-4 paragraphs long, depending on how much your teacher has instructed you to write.

Key features of the body:

  • This is the chance for you to go into as much detail as possible about what happened.

  • The most important information about the events should go first.

  • Each paragraph should be on a slightly different aspect of what happened.

  • It should be written in the third person.

  • Explain the background information that is relevant to the story.

  • Include evidence, facts, and quotes from people related to the event.

  • You could also include a quote from an expert on the topic you are reporting on.

The Tail:

The tail includes the least important information from your report and functions, to sum up the events.

Key features of the tail:

  • Add any extra or surrounding information about the event or related topics.

  • Include links for where to find extra information about the topic or other news reports.

  • You could also feature a quote from an expert or witness, to sum up the story or imply what may unfold next.

- Writing the Headline

A news story is not complete without a headline. It serves as the title of your article. It is the first thing that readers see and helps them decide whether they want to read the full story. Headlines are crucial for attracting attention, providing context, and guiding readers to the content that follows. In journalism, a headline is considered an essential element of a news story, and its absence can leave the story lacking in clarity and impact.

Rules in Writing Headline

  • Must have a verb

  • Do not use period (.)

  • Use a comma in place of “and” and a semicolon to separate sentences

  • Don’t use the articles: a, an, the

  • Don’t use names unless the person is well-known

  • Use the infinitive for future events

  • Use specific terms instead of generalities

  • The first letter of the first word and proper nouns are capitalized

  • The headline should give the gist of the story in just a few words

  • The content of the headline over a news story should be taken from the lead of a news

  • Write numbers in Arabic form (1, 2, 3, 4, 5…)

  • Use single quotation marks instead of double quotation marks

  • Use only familiar abbreviations or acronyms in the headline

How to Write an Effective Headline

  • Must be correct (in fact and implication)

  • Must correct to ordinary readers

  • Must attract attention

  • Must set (or match) the tone of the article

After the headline, you must consider including the byline. A byline is a part of an article that tells the reader who is the author or writer of an article in a publication and is placed below the headline. Bylines are essential in news writing as they enhance credibility, promote transparency, hold writers accountable, give recognition to journalists, and establish a connection with readers.

- General Rules for News Writing

  • Do not pass a verdict or make a judgment;

  • Do not editorialize: Leave editorializing to the editorial writers. News reports must be as straightforward and objective as possible;

  • Avoid the use of unfamiliar jargon;

  • Keep yourself and other reporters out of the story;

  • Avoid the use of “told me,” “told this writer” or “told our reporter;

  • There must be perfect accuracy in writing and reporting;

  • There must be no willful distortion of facts in either news or headlines;

  • Always use simple words;

  • Leave at least a one-inch margin at the left, right, top, and bottom of each page: Do not overcrowd the pages;

  • You must quote accurately;

  • Use “said” if you want to write in the present tense and, “said that” if it is reported speech: This style helps the copy retain currency and life;

  • Always read your copy carefully and connect all typographical spelling and grammatical mistakes before turning it in;

  • Use only one thought or idea per paragraph; and

  • Write to inform and to express, not to impress.

2. Feature Writing

— Feature Writing is a form of literary expression within the field of journalism that is based on true stories or facts that aim to provide more comprehensive information.

— It focuses on in-depth storytelling, analysis, and exploration of a particular topic, person, or event to engage and inform readers and it is widely used in magazines, online media, and newspapers.

— Its purpose is to entertain and inform the audience or readers by highlighting an interesting and engaging angle of a straight news story in a creative and unique perspective.

- News Story vs. Feature

A feature story takes a different style in delivering facts and details than a news story. They differ in tone, structure, purpose, timing, and even length. In writing, a feature writer has much more freedom and can be more reflective than a straight news writer. - Structure of Feature Writing

Like any form of writing, a feature story follows a standard structure. While the structure may change based on the topic, a feature article should always consist of a headline, introduction, main content, and a conclusion.

Structure of the Feature Story (Hawthorne, 2024)

Title & Headline

The headline performs two important functions. An effective headline:

  • Grabs the reader's attention and persuades them to read the article

  • Highlights the main idea of the article.

  • Includes keywords (for online articles).

Lead

  • It should create an image, send a verbal message and capture the reader's imagination.

  • The tone should fit the mood of the story and supply the theme or angle.

  • It should lead the reader into the story.

What makes a good lead:

  • An anecdote that represents the universal truth.

  • The description of a scene that helps to establish a tone or mood.

  • A description of a subject: face, demeanor, posture.

  • A powerful quote.

  • A startling statement.

  • The strength of any sentence is judged by its degree of improbability. "Fall is here and that means the start of football season," tells the reader nothing.

  • The following lead is much better: "Fall is here, a fact that head coach Bill Smith may view with some trepidation. Not only does he have to replace 14 seniors from a 2-8 season, he must deal with the recent season-ending injury to returning quarterback Dave Jones."

Body

The headline performs two important functions. An effective headline:

  • It should be unified with all material relating to the theme. All unnecessary data should be omitted. Care should be taken to make certain that each sentence and paragraph adds to the impact of the narrative.

  • The story is organized in a logical order. This does not necessarily mean chronological order. However, the reader should be able to follow the narrative of the story as the action jumps back and forth from the past, the present, and even the future.

Conclusion

The headline performs two important functions. An effective headline:

  • The ending should give a sense of finality and resolution to the reader. It wraps up all loose ends and leaves the reader with a single, significant thought. It stands alone as the reader's final impression.

- Different Types of Feature Writing

As cited in an article by Leverage Edu, 2021, The book "The Universal Journalist" by British journalist David Randall, a widely praised work on journalism, discusses various types of feature stories. These are:

  • Colour Piece: A feature story that essentially tries to inform readers regarding a particular theme or subject.

  • Fly on the Wall: A feature story that is conceived and narrated unobtrusively and mostly without the explicit permission of the subjects.

  • Behind the Scenes: A feature story that shifts its focus from the principal event to the background and narrates an interesting tale.

  • In Disguise: A feature story that is told while the storyteller is a part of the event.

  • Interview: A feature story that develops itself around questions asked to a respondent, who is usually in a place of prominence.

  • Profile: A feature story that is based on the exploits of a particular eminent person with or without his/ her interview.

  • How-To: A feature story that is dependent on research and helps readers in solving a problem or deciphering a scenario.

  • Fact Box/ Chronology: A feature story that provides plain and simple facts mostly in chronological order.

  • Backgrounder/ A History of: A feature story that provides detailed information.

  • Full Texts: A feature story that is nothing but extracts from a book or transcripts of an interview.

  • Testimony: A feature story that is the first-person account of an individual.

  • Analysis: A feature story that scholarly analyzes an event.

  • Vox Pop/ Expert Roundup: A feature story that accumulates opinions from the general citizenry and thought leaders concerning a subject

  • Opinion Poll: A feature story that conducts research on opinions and presents a generalized summary of the accumulated opinions

  • Review: A feature story that reviews a work of art and presents a generalized opinion.

- Tips for Writing Feature Article

Feature writing demands careful observation of insightful interviewing, going behind the scenes, and commitment to uncovering the big story beneath the facts. Here are the following tips that can help to write a feature article effectively: (aaft.com/blog)

  • Choose an Interesting Angle:

    The first step in feature story writing is selecting a unique and compelling angle or theme for your story. Look for an aspect of the topic that hasn’t been explored widely, or find a fresh perspective that can pique readers’ curiosity.

  • Conduct Thorough Research:

    Solid research is the foundation of any feature story. Dive deep into your subject matter, interview relevant sources, and gather as much information as possible. Understand your subject inside out to present a comprehensive and accurate portrayal.

  • Humanize Your Story:

    Feature stories often revolve around people, their experiences, and their emotions. Humanize your narrative by introducing relatable characters and sharing their stories, struggles, and triumphs.

  • Create a Strong Lead:

    Your opening paragraph, or lead, should be attention-grabbing and set the tone for the entire story. Engage your readers from the start with an anecdote, a thought-provoking question, or a vivid description.

  • Structure Your Story:

    Feature stories typically follow a narrative structure with a beginning, middle, and end. The beginning introduces the topic and engages the reader, the middle explores the depth of the subject, and the end provides closure or leaves readers with something to ponder.

  • Use Descriptive Language:

    Paint a vivid picture with your words. Utilize descriptive language and sensory details to transport your readers into the world you’re depicting.

  • Incorporate Quotes and Anecdotes:

    Quotes from interviews and anecdotes from your research can breathe life into your story. They add authenticity and provide insights from real people.

  • Engage Emotionally :

    Feature stories should evoke emotions. Whether it’s empathy, curiosity, joy, or sadness, aim to connect with your readers on a personal level.

3. Sports Writing

Sports Writing is a specialized form of journalism that covers sports, athletes, or other sports-related issues and events. Sports journalism aims to inform, entertain, and engage readers by capturing the excitement, drama, and human-interest aspects of sports. A journalist who reports on sports is called a sportswriter. A sportswriter is responsible for crafting an engaging and descriptive narrative of sports events, statistics, and interviews, and providing analysis and reports on game outcomes.

In writing a sports article, a writer must have an interest in all sports events. They should know the rules and regulations of the event or game they are writing about. They should have a working knowledge of the language and jargon of sports writing usually referred to as sports lingo.

- Qualities of a Good Sports Writer

  • Must know sports; rules, strategy, team, and players’ records;

  • Must attend the games or meets as a reporter, not as spectator or cheerer;

  • Must know coaches and players as intimately as possible;

  • Must observe accuracy;

  • Must be able to take notes quickly without losing the sequence of the play;

  • Must be fair and unbiased in reporting events, although he has a favorite team;

  • Must not make comments without supporting them with facts on which they are based, although sports writers have more freedom than any other news writers;

  • Must use the specialized language of the particular sports that he is reporting;

  • Must avoid sports slang and trite expressions which hack writers use today. They should tell the story in plain factual terms that describe the action; and

  • Must bear in mind that they write for their readers and not for themselves.

Structure of the Sport Story

Lead

it summarizes or analyzes the event by giving the significance, the winner, the hero, the score, the place, and the time or date when the event happens.

Classic or Summary Lead

(1) Who won (2) against whom (3) by what score (4) where and (5) when.

Example:

(1) The Rizal National High School won against (2) Llanera High School, (3) 87-53, (4) at the opponent’s ground (5) yesterday afternoon.

Climax of the Game

it is the highlight of the game. This part of the story accounts for the aggressiveness of the team or the player on the court.

Decisive Play

This accounts for the most crucial part of the game that gives a certain team the winning margin.

Quote

It is important to account for the sentiments of the hero/es of the day. Words fresh from the winner/s are vital in the writing of the story of posterity.

Play-by-play Account

This part of the story recaps the sports event covered. This is usually done through round-by-round reporting. It is important to note that this part must be score-oriented and must describe the progress of the fight in detail.

Significance of the Game

consider questions like, Was a champion at stake? Do the standings of the teams change? Who gets the cup? Is the grudge wiped out? (Championship, do-or-die elimination).

Other Information

this might include a comparison of the teams, individual stars, weather conditions, or crowd and celebration.

- Basic Rules for Writing Sports Story

  • Know the rules of the game

  • Use special vocabulary (Sports Terms or Sports Lingo)

  • Get all your facts

  • Jot down an outline

  • Make a good lead

  • Use punchy verbs or strong verbs

  • Get quotations from players, coaches, officials, and spectators

  • Include key plays (best players)

  • Revise or edit your sports stories

- Dynamic and Punchy Verbs

  • Win by a big margin: battered, mangled, clobbered, powdered, walloped, pulverized

  • Win by a small margin: shaded, eked out, nipped, edged out

  • Defeat a champion: dethroned, overthrew, overturned, toppled, snatched the crown, grabbed the crown, bagged the title

  • Remain the title: kept the crown, retained the title

  • Give an opponent a zero score: blanked, whitewashed
